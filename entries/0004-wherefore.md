# wherefore

## Description

A compound interrogative of the *where-* lineage, asking after causes:
*wherefore* means *for what reason*, and a sentence containing it is a
request for an explanation, not a location. The organism is large for a
*Particula* — two full syllables, front-loaded with stress — and was in
its breeding centuries among the most direct instruments English had for
demanding that someone account for themselves.

It is documented here for a distinction no other organism in this guide
yet holds: it is best known from a sighting in which it is misidentified.
The most famous *wherefore* in the language is almost universally read as
a *where*, a circumstance treated at length under *Easily Confused With*
and one the Society records without satisfaction, since the error is
made by readers and the organism is in no position to correct them.

## Status

**Extinct in the Wild.** No breeding population has been recorded in
unrehearsed speech or unselfconscious prose for well over a century. The
species survives in three captive populations: (1) the legal pleading,
where it heads the prayer for relief and is maintained at public expense;
(2) the fossil noun in the fixed phrase *the whys and wherefores*, where
it persists by having stopped being an interrogative at all; (3) a single
line of Elizabethan drama, restocked nightly by the world's repertory
theatres. The Society had hoped this entry would be the guide's first
pressing of a fully Extinct species. It reports with some care that it
is not — the captive stock is genuinely alive — and refers the reader to
the official vocabulary, which exists for exactly this discipline.

## Range

Formerly bred throughout English speech and prose; the 1611 Authorized
Version alone contains dozens of breeding records (*wherefore didst thou
doubt?*), indicating a population then near saturation. Contraction was
rapid through the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries and total by the
twentieth.

Current captive range: court filings, where it appears at the head of the
concluding paragraph, customarily in full capitals; contracts drafted by
firms that bill by the hour; the theatre; and the noun preserve noted
under Status. Strays occur in historical fiction and in the speech of
persons who have recently left a production of *Romeo and Juliet*.

## Call

Two syllables, trochaic, stress on the first: **WHERE**-fore. In its
captive legal habitat the call is rendered entirely in capitals —
*WHEREFORE, Plaintiff respectfully prays* — which the Society interprets
as display rather than volume, the organism having no competitors left
to shout over. On stage the call is produced with the stress migrated
unnaturally toward the second syllable by actors signalling that the
word is old, a distortion not found in any historical recording and
best regarded as an artefact of captivity.

## Sign

A text the organism has passed through will show: (1) archaic verbal
plumage in the immediate vicinity — *didst*, *hast*, *art* — since
*wherefore* has not travelled with modern conjugations in living memory;
(2) in legal habitat, a following comma and a verb of petition (*prays*,
*requests*, *demands*); (3) an answer, somewhere in the following lines,
that begins with *because*. This last is the surest sign and the basis
of the field test given below.

## Easily Confused With

**Where.** This is the field mark, and the confusion is not hypothetical:
it is the dominant reading of the most famous sentence the species
appears in. *O Romeo, Romeo, wherefore art thou Romeo?* asks why he is
Romeo — why, of all the names in Verona, the one her family is at war
with — and is answered two lines later by an argument about names. It
does not ask where he is. He is beneath the balcony.

The field test is the shape of the answer. A *wherefore* takes an answer
beginning with *because*; a *where* takes an answer beginning with a
preposition. Productions in which Juliet delivers the line while
visibly searching the garden have documented a *where*; no *wherefore*
was present.

The Society notes the resulting condition of the species without
parallel in this guide: an organism extinct in the wild, surviving
chiefly in a single preserved utterance, and famous in that utterance
for being mistaken for its commonest living relative. The relative, for
its part, has never acknowledged the resemblance.

## Breeding & Hybridization

*Wherefore* belongs to a once-productive breeding population of *where-*
compounds: *whereby*, *wherein*, *whereof*, *whereupon*, *whereas*,
*wherewith* and its derivative *wherewithal*. The lineage bred by simple
suffixion and at its peak could attach *where-* to nearly any preposition
in reach.

The siblings' fates are instructive. Those that survived did so by one
of two strategies: taking salaried positions in legal and contractual
habitat (*whereas*, which now opens recitals and does little else), or
abandoning the interrogative life entirely and going noun (*wherewithal*;
*wherefore* itself, in the plural, in *the whys and wherefores*). No
member of the lineage survived in the wild on its original diet.

Continental cousins remain abundant — Dutch *waarvoor*, German *wofür* —
and breed freely in everyday speech, a circumstance the Society offers
to any reader inclined to believe the extinction was inevitable.

## History of Classification

A Middle English compound of *hwǣr* and *for*, assembled during the
period when English built its question-words the way it built its barns,
from materials on hand. Its decline is attributed not to habitat loss
but to direct competition: the native *why*, occupying an identical
niche at half the metabolic cost, outcompeted it in every register
informal enough to care about effort. The pattern — a two-syllable
specialist displaced by a one-syllable generalist — recurs throughout
the historical record and the Society expects to document it again.

The binomial assigns the species to its surviving habitat (*forensis*,
of the forum). A proposal regarding the epithet is lodged in the
Marginalia.
