# petrichor

## Description

A mid-sized sensory noun of unusually well-documented origin: bred in
captivity by two Australian researchers in 1964, from Greek stock
(*petra*, stone; *ichor*, the fluid in the veins of gods), for the
specific purpose of naming the smell of rain on dry ground. It is among
the very few words whose hatching was recorded in a peer-reviewed journal
(*Nature*, 7 March 1964), making it a rare specimen of successful captive
breeding followed by deliberate release.

The organism is notable for its plumage-to-payload ratio. It carries one
modest meaning in an unusually ornate body, a trait that has made it
beloved of collectors and suspicious to working writers.

## Status

**Naturalized.** Escaped the technical literature in the late twentieth
century via science journalism and is now established across all
English-speaking territories. Several regional observers report the
population has exceeded carrying capacity; see Marginalia.

## Range

Breeds year-round in poetry, perfume marketing, and dating profiles,
where it displays. Winters in trivia. Strays have been recorded in
weather broadcasts and at least one prestige-television monologue.
Conspicuously absent from actual meteorology, which prefers the
unglamorous native species *humidity* and *convection*.

## Call

Three syllables, trochaic onset, stress on the first: **PET**-ri-chor.
A faint smugness is audible in most field recordings, particularly when
the organism is produced immediately after rain in the presence of an
audience. Juveniles are sometimes heard with a soft *ch* (as in *church*);
the Society regards this as a regional dialect rather than an error,
over the strong objection of one founding member.

## Sign

Distinctive. A text the organism has passed through will show: (1) a
nearby rain event, (2) a slight rise in the register of the surrounding
sentences, as if they have straightened their posture, and (3) the
displaced tracks of the older native phrase *the smell of rain*, which it
outcompetes for the same niche despite being, by weight, less nutritious.

## Easily Confused With

Actually having smelled the rain. The two co-occur less often than the
organism's frequency would suggest. In the field, the reliable
distinguishing mark is direction of attention: the experience points at
the ground; the word points at the speaker.

## History of Classification

Unlike most species in this guide, whose lineages must be reconstructed
from fossil spellings, *petrichor* has papers. Bear & Thomas, 1964,
working from the oils that dry earth absorbs from plants and releases in
rain. The Society notes with approval that the word for the smell was
created by the people who isolated the chemistry — a rare case of the
poets and the scientists being the same individuals, which may account
for the organism's hybrid temperament: laboratory bones, ornamental
feathers.
