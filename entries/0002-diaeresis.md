# diaeresis

## Description

A diacritical *Signum* of separating habit. When applied to the second of
two adjacent vowels, it refuses their merger into a single syllable:
*coöperate*, *naïve*, *reëlect*. The organism does not change what either
vowel is; it insists only that they decline to combine. It is among the
smallest of the *Signum*, and the most easily mistaken for an organism
whose habits are entirely different — see *Easily Confused With*.

The diaeresis is a separator that has, through overwhelming territorial
loss, become known mostly by its absence.

## Status

**Critically Endangered.** Survives in fragmented habitat, principally one
weekly periodical in the United States and a small number of personal
names actively defended by their bearers. The cause of decline is
mechanical — Linotype cost, then keyboard convenience, then autocorrect —
and not semantic. The need it served has not gone away; the readers have
adapted by re-reading, which the Society regards as a less elegant
solution.

## Range

Breeds reliably in three habitats: (1) the house style of one American
weekly, where it is bred in captivity and where its observers may be
counted; (2) the loanword preserves — *naïve*, *Noël*, *aiguë* — where it
persists as a holdover from French manners; (3) the diaereses of certain
proper names, where it survives by being part of the deed. Strays appear
in linguistics textbooks and opera programs. Conspicuously absent from
text-message corpora and search-engine queries, where it is stripped on
entry by mechanisms that do not announce themselves.

## Call

Silent — *Signum* are mute by definition. Its prosodic effect, however,
is audible in the host word: a hairline syllable boundary inserted where
most contemporary readers produce a glide. The boundary is brief and
entirely deniable. A reader who pronounces it has been trained to; a
reader who does not has not been told.

## Sign

The diaeresis-host is recognized by three marks: (1) two points presented
over the second vowel of a pair, never the first; (2) the absence of the
more aggressive cousins (acute, grave, circumflex), with which the
diaeresis declines to share a host; (3) a faint sense, in the surrounding
paragraph, of a publication that has paid for a proofreader. The
diaeresis travels with capital expenditure and a certain managerial
restraint.

## Easily Confused With

**The umlaut.** This is the field mark that matters. The two organisms
are visually indistinguishable and functionally unrelated — a case of
convergent typography, the marsupial wolf and the placental wolf wearing
identical pelts for different reasons. The umlaut *transforms* a vowel
into a different vowel (German *a* → *ä* is not a slowed *a* but a wholly
other sound). The diaeresis transforms nothing; it merely refuses a
merger. The umlaut acts; the diaeresis abstains.

In the field: if the host word is German, suspect umlaut. If the host
word is English and the dots stand over the second of a pair, suspect
diaeresis. If the host word is *Brontë*, the Society has fought over it
for a century and a half and recommends moving on.

A subtler confusion is with the **hyphen** in *co-operate*, which is not
a sibling organism but a competitor. The hyphen colonised the diaeresis's
niche during the twentieth century and was subsequently displaced from it
by the closed compound (*cooperate*), leaving the niche unoccupied. The
diaeresis has not returned to fill it; the readers, as noted, re-read
instead.

## History of Classification

The mark predates its English career. Greek grammarians used it to
indicate vowel separation in scansion; Latin epigraphers borrowed it;
French typesetters domesticated it. It entered English via loanword and
was, in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, briefly common
in serious American prose. Linotype made it expensive. Hyphenation took
its work, and then the closed compound took the hyphen's, leaving the
diaeresis with no employer and a reputation for fastidiousness. Its
principal remaining patron is noted under Status.
