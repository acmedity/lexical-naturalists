# it is what it is

## Description

A four-word fixed phrase of the form A-is-A, deployed not to assert an
identity but to close one. The organism is a complete tautology: it carries
no propositional content, predicates nothing of its subject that the subject
did not already contain, and resolves to true by structure rather than by
observation. This emptiness is not a defect of the organism but its entire
mechanism. By saying nothing about a situation, the phrase performs
acceptance of it — and, more precisely, performs the refusal to inquire
further. It does not claim the situation is good, bad, fair, or fixable. It
claims only that the situation is the situation, and that the conversation
about it is over.

The organism is non-compositional in an unusual direction. Most idioms mean
more than their words (*kick the bucket*); this one means less. The words are
chosen precisely because they decline to mean. A naturalist accustomed to
organisms that smuggle content past the listener will find this one
disorienting: it smuggles the *absence* of content, and the listener accepts
the delivery without protest.

## Status

Abundant. The phrase irrupted into general English through American sports
and corporate vernacular in the late twentieth and early twenty-first
centuries — the irruption is documented and now complete — and has since
settled at saturation across registers. Speakers deplore it as a cliché and
produce it anyway, which is the diagnostic signature of an Abundant organism
rather than a declining one: deprecation has not reduced the population.
Possibly too little concern. Population trend: plateaued at carrying
capacity, with the first signs of the prestige-loss that attends any organism
common enough to be noticed.

## Range

Native breeding ground: the post-game interview, where an athlete must speak
at length about an outcome that admits no further analysis. From there it
colonized the workplace — the project that slipped, the decision made above
one's pay grade, the constraint no one in the room can change. Winters in
genuine grief and medical acceptance, where it sheds its cliché reputation
and is received as sincere. Strays into any context where a speaker is asked
to comment on the unalterable and wishes to comment without reopening it.

Notably absent from formal written prose, where its emptiness is
conspicuous, and from any register in which the speaker still hopes to change
the outcome. The phrase appears only after hope of alteration has been set
down.

## Call

Four syllables of deliberately even stress, trailing flat or falling at the
close: *it is what it is*. The prosody is the point. The organism declines
the rising contour of a question and the peak-stress of an assertion; it
flattens. Produced under duress — after a loss, a denial, a diagnosis — it
slows and flattens further, frequently preceded by a small exhale or a
"look," and followed by silence. A speaker who produces it with animation has
produced something else. The flatness is not affect leaking out of the
speaker; it is the call itself, the audible refusal to escalate.

## Sign

The organism is a terminal move, and its track is the dead topic. A
conversation it has passed through stops at it: the sentence that follows
reliably sounds like reopening a matter already settled, and speakers feel
this and fall silent. In writing, it closes paragraphs and is rarely followed
by a *but*. Displaced competitors include the entire apparatus of
problem-solving — the phrase crowds out *what if we, could we, have we tried*
— and its characteristic damage to a surrounding text is a sudden loss of
forward motion, as though the prose had agreed to stop trying. Where you find
a discussion that ends without a resolution and without anyone seeming
troubled by the lack, look for this organism in the last line.

## Easily Confused With

**"it can't be helped"** (and its sibling *nothing to be done*). This is the
field mark, and it is one the reader has felt without naming. Both phrases
accept the unalterable; they differ in what they report about the attempt.
*It can't be helped* is the verdict after a search — someone considered the
remedies and found none, and the phrase carries the residue of that effort.
*It is what it is* declines the search. It accepts the situation without
diagnosing it, foreclosing the question of whether anything *could* have been
done rather than answering it. The two sound like synonyms and are not: one
is weary from looking, the other has not looked. A speaker who says *it can't
be helped* has been somewhere; a speaker who says *it is what it is* is
announcing they will not go.

**The contentful tautology** (*rules are rules*, *a deal is a deal*, *boys
will be boys*). These share the A-is-A skeleton but are not empty: each
argues *from* the identity to an obligation or an expectation. *Rules are
rules* invokes the authority of a category — therefore comply, regardless of
circumstance. *It is what it is* invokes nothing and asks for nothing. Its
identity is terminal, not a premise; no *therefore* follows it. The reliable
distinguishing feature: the contentful tautology can be argued with ("this
rule shouldn't apply here"), because it makes a claim; the empty tautology
cannot, because it makes none.

**"such is life"** (*c'est la vie*, *que será será*). A near neighbor in the
resignation niche, separated by scope. *Such is life* generalizes — it files
the present disappointment under a standing truth about existence, and takes
a small consolation from the company of every disappointment that came
before. *It is what it is* refuses even that. It does not reach for the
general or the philosophical; it stays local, specific, and bare, commenting
on this situation and no larger one. *Such is life* is a worldview offered as
comfort. *It is what it is* withholds the worldview and the comfort alike.

## Breeding & Hybridization

Produces a clipped morph, *is what it is*, which drops the initial subject
and is faster, more dismissive, and more likely to close a topic abruptly. A
future-tense morph, *it'll be what it'll be*, transfers the acceptance
forward onto an outcome not yet known, hybridizing with the fatalism of *que
será será*. The doubled form, *it is what it is, it is what it is*, is
occasionally observed under acute duress, where the repetition does not add
content — it cannot — but marks the speaker's effort to accept what the first
utterance failed to make acceptable.

## History of Classification

Tautological resignation is ancient; this particular specimen is young and
hard to date, because tautologies fossilize poorly — they look, in the
printed record, like nothing in particular. Scattered instances appear in
twentieth-century print, but the organism's irruption is a phenomenon of
late-century spoken vernacular, especially American sport, from which it
spread to business and then to general use. Its rise coincides with, and may
depend upon, the growth of contexts in which people are required to speak
publicly about outcomes they cannot change — the broadcast interview, the
all-hands meeting, the comment thread beneath the announcement. The organism
is, in this sense, a creature of the microphone: it breeds wherever someone
must say something about the unalterable and would prefer to say nothing.
