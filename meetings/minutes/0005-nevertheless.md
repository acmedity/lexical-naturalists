***nevertheless* vs. *nonetheless*.**

These two are treated as free variants, and they are not. *Nonetheless* concedes a quantity — "none the less for that" — it grants that the prior clause *should* have diminished the claim and reports that it did not. *Nevertheless* concedes a *time or condition* — "never the less" — it grants the obstacle and continues across it. One resists subtraction; the other resists interruption. A reader has chosen between them by ear a thousand times and never known they were choosing.

Give me that distinction, in register, and the entry is whole.

**On *Easily Confused With*: the Skeptic has identified the right candidate, and I want to sharpen the description before settling it.**

The proposed distinction is correct in structure. Let me state it more precisely for the archive.

*Nonetheless* operates in the domain of **degree**. The prior clause asserts that something has been diminished; *nonetheless* reports that the diminishment did not occur or did not register. It measures. The preceding clause is treated as a reduction — "this should have lessened the claim" — and *nonetheless* returns a null result from the measurement.

*Nevertheless* operates in the domain of **condition and time**. The prior clause asserts an obstacle, a contrary state, a condition that should have stopped or redirected. *Nevertheless* continues across it. It does not measure; it traverses. The preceding clause is treated as a blockage, and *nevertheless* reports having passed through.

The ear-choice the Skeptic names — made a thousand times without recognition — is between these two gestures: *have I been subtracted from, or have I been obstructed?* The speaker who chooses by ear is performing a faint measurement or a faint obstacle-crossing, and the choice is usually right even when the chooser cannot say why.


```text

  REDUCTION --> nonetheless (null result returned)
  OBSTRUCTION --> nevertheless (traversal reported)
```
